\section{Risk Mitigation and Policy Implications}
\label{sec:mitigation}

The quantitative findings in Sections~\ref{sec:intro} and~\ref{sec:impact} point to
three distinct layers of structural risk: overextended hyperscaler balance sheets, a
synchronised IPO supply shock, and the amplification effect of elevated index
concentration. Each layer suggests a corresponding mitigation strategy. We organise
this section around structural market reforms, compute market innovations, and
investor-level hedging overlays.

\subsection{The Compute Commoditisation Pressure Valve}

Before examining instrument-level hedges, it is worth examining the most important
natural stabiliser in this system: the commoditisation of AI compute itself.

GPU hardware prices have historically declined at rates consistent with a modified
Moore's Law curve. If compute prices fall sharply (for example, because of NVIDIA's
Blackwell and next-generation architecture releases, or because of competing chip
manufacturers gaining market share), the economic justification for continued Capex
at the 32.4\% CAGR trajectory weakens considerably. Hyperscalers would face a choice:
continue building at current rates despite falling per-unit ROI, or slow deployment
and allow FCF to recover.

History suggests they will initially continue. Cisco did not voluntarily reduce capital
expenditure during the fibre boom; the market forced the correction. However, the speed
of compute commoditisation creates a genuine pressure valve. A 30\% decline in H100
rental rates over 12 months (consistent with observed CoreWeave spot pricing trends in
early 2026) would reduce the effective Capex-to-FCF stress ratio by an estimated
0.4 turns, partially offsetting the structural divergence documented in Section~\ref{sec:intro}.

\subsection{The Ornn Compute Price Index and Transparent Reference Rates}

The central prerequisite for a functioning compute derivatives market is a transparent,
standardised reference rate for spot GPU pricing. Currently, CoreWeave, Lambda Labs,
Microsoft Azure, and Google Cloud all publish different prices for equivalent H100
compute capacity, creating fragmented price discovery. This is precisely the market
structure problem that preceded the development of LIBOR for short-term interest rates
and Henry Hub for natural gas.

The Ornn Compute Price Index (OCPI) is a proposed standardised benchmark that
aggregates GPU spot prices across major providers into a single reference rate,
weighted by daily transaction volume. Under the OCPI framework:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The benchmark is calculated daily from observed transaction data across at
        least three independent compute providers.
  \item Prices are normalised to a standard unit (e.g., \$/H100-GPU-hour at 80\% SLA
        uptime) to ensure cross-provider comparability.
  \item A published forward curve (30, 60, 90, and 180-day tenors) provides the basis
        for derivatives pricing.
\end{itemize}

An OCPI-based reference rate would allow hyperscalers, hedge funds, and institutional
investors to enter into binding financial contracts linked to the cost of AI compute
capacity, independent of any single cloud provider's pricing decisions.

\subsection{ICE GPU Futures: Hedging Hyperscaler Hardware Exposure}

Once a transparent reference rate exists, exchange-traded futures contracts become
viable. ICE (Intercontinental Exchange) GPU futures, structured analogously to energy
commodity futures, would allow:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Hyperscaler hedging:} A hyperscaler committing to \$80 billion in GPU
        procurement over the next 12 months can enter into short futures positions on
        the OCPI forward curve, locking in today's compute price and insulating its
        FCF projections from hardware cost inflation.
  \item \textbf{Speculative positioning:} Macro hedge funds holding the view that AI
        compute demand will slow (consistent with the Systemic Short scenario) can
        express that view through short GPU futures without taking direct equity risk.
  \item \textbf{Arbitrage between hardware and equity:} If GPU futures prices imply a
        different demand trajectory than Nasdaq-100 valuations, that spread creates an
        arbitrage opportunity that pulls both markets toward consistent pricing.
\end{itemize}

The practical challenge is clearing and settlement. Physical delivery of GPU hardware is
impractical at futures contract scale. A cash-settled structure keyed to the OCPI
published fixing is the technically sound solution, analogous to the cash settlement
mechanism used in oil futures and interest rate swaps.

Figure~\ref{fig:gpu_hedge} quantifies the hedging benefit under the Systemic Short
scenario (30\% OCPI spot crash at month 7). A 300{,}000-unit H100 fleet with
\$9B of Capex exposure, hedged via short ICE GPU futures at the M1 forward rate,
preserves \$528.6M of cumulative margin over 12 months that would otherwise be
destroyed by the spot price collapse.

\subsection{Kalshi Prediction Markets: Retail and Institutional Timeline Hedging}

Prediction markets offer a complementary, lower-friction mechanism for hedging specific
event outcomes within the IPO timeline. Kalshi (regulated by the CFTC as a designated
contract market) currently lists binary outcome contracts on a range of financial events.

For the 2026 IPO liquidity vacuum thesis, the relevant prediction market instruments would be:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{IPO timing contracts:} Binary yes/no contracts on whether SpaceX, Anthropic,
        or OpenAI complete a public market listing before specified quarterly deadlines.
        Institutional investors can hedge their exposure to liquidity vacuum timing risk by
        purchasing ``yes'' contracts on early listings (increasing supply shock probability)
        or ``no'' contracts as a delay hedge.
  \item \textbf{Valuation range contracts:} Contracts paying out if the opening-day
        market capitalisation of a listed entity falls within a specified range
        (e.g., below \$500B, \$500B--\$1T, above \$1T). These allow precise calibration
        of the float drain magnitude uncertainty discussed in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.
  \item \textbf{NDX drawdown threshold contracts:} Binary contracts on whether the
        Nasdaq-100 closes more than 20\% below its June 2026 peak before year-end.
        These provide a direct, CFTC-regulated hedge against the Systemic Short scenario
        at a fraction of the cost of options on futures.
\end{itemize}

The liquidity limitations of current prediction markets mean that institutional-scale
positions cannot be easily established. However, the price signal itself (implied
probability of the Systemic Short scenario) provides valuable real-time information
that can be used to dynamically adjust the scenario weights in the PFE model.

\subsection{Index Provider Concentration Guardrails}

S\&P Dow Jones Indices and Nasdaq currently apply concentration rebalancing when any
single constituent exceeds 24\% of index weight, or when the top-5 collectively exceed
50\% \citep{sp500methodology,nasdaq100methodology}. Both thresholds were exceeded by
mid-2024. The existing rules are reactive and infrequently triggered. A more robust
framework would use continuous HHI monitoring:

\begin{itemize}
  \item When index HHI exceeds 800, trigger a scheduled quarterly review with mandatory
        weight redistribution if HHI remains above threshold for two consecutive quarters.
  \item Cap the concentration amplifier $\alpha_{\HHI}$ at $1.5\times$ by design,
        reducing the worst-case tail PFE by an estimated 35--40\%.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Regulatory Circuit-Breaker Enhancements}

The Systemic Short scenario becomes self-reinforcing above a $-20\%$ peak-to-trough
NDX drawdown. At that threshold, margin calls from leveraged ETF products generate
forced selling at an estimated \$8 billion per day, exceeding the institutional
market-making buffer at normal spread widths.

We recommend that the SEC and CFTC establish coordinated circuit-breaker protocols
for AI-sector-specific drawdowns, distinct from the current market-wide thresholds
calibrated to S\&P~500 moves:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Level 1 (NDX $-15\%$):} Mandatory disclosure filing for any entity with
        net short notional exceeding \$500 million in Nasdaq-100-linked products.
  \item \textbf{Level 2 (NDX $-20\%$):} 30-minute trading halt in leveraged NDX ETFs
        and single-stock options on top-5 AI constituents.
  \item \textbf{Level 3 (NDX $-30\%$):} Full session halt in AI-cluster derivatives,
        with mandatory next-day position reporting for all registered swap dealers.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Investor-Level Hedging Overlays}

For institutional investors with significant AI equity exposure, three practical
hedging structures follow directly from the VaR and PFE framework:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Long-dated NDX put spreads:} Purchasing 18-month at-the-money NDX
        put options, funded by selling 40\% out-of-the-money puts, creates a
        cost-efficient protection structure covering the Systemic Short scenario
        ($-30\%$ threshold) without full premium outlay.

  \item \textbf{VIX call spreads:} The 2022 drawdown demonstrated that implied
        volatility on the Nasdaq reached 35--45 at peak stress from a pre-shock level
        of 18--22. A VIX call spread (long 30, short 55) structured for the Q3--Q4
        2026 window provides convex payoff precisely during the anticipated IPO
        supply shock window.

  \item \textbf{Sector rotation into compute-infrastructure beneficiaries:}
        Utilities and industrial REITs with data-centre power grid exposure
        (for example, Vistra Energy and NRG) benefit from hyperscaler Capex regardless
        of whether AI applications reach commercial inflection, providing a long
        position with a structurally different risk profile.
\end{enumerate}
