\section{Market Impact: Index Concentration and Tail Risk}
\label{sec:impact}

\subsection{Index Concentration and the HHI Amplifier}

When a small number of stocks dominate an index, any shock to that cluster propagates
more aggressively through the index than the individual stock-level move would suggest.
The mechanism is passive vehicle rebalancing: index-tracking ETFs and mutual funds must
sell in proportion to their weighting, amplifying the initial price signal across the
entire portfolio. We measure this concentration using the
Herfindahl-Hirschman Index (HHI), which sums the squared market share weights of the
top-5 constituents.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig7_concentration_hhi.png}
  \caption{
    \textbf{Index Concentration: HHI and Top-5 Weight (2010--2026).}
    The HHI for the S\&P~500 top-5 concentration rose from 297 in 2010 to 874 in 2026
    ($2.78\times$ amplifier). For the Nasdaq-100, HHI rose from 534 to 1{,}418
    ($2.66\times$). Source: interpolated from published S\&P and Nasdaq index weight
    disclosures \citep{sp500methodology,nasdaq100methodology}.
  }
  \label{fig:hhi}
\end{figure}

We define the HHI-based concentration amplifier $\alpha_{\HHI}$ as the ratio of the
current HHI to a 2010 baseline (a period of normal concentration):

\begin{equation}
  \alpha_{\HHI} = \frac{\HHI_{2026}}{\HHI_{2010}}
  \label{eq:hhi_amplifier}
\end{equation}

For the Nasdaq-100, $\alpha_{\HHI}^{\text{NDX}} = 1418 / 534 = 2.66$. This factor scales
the tail-risk estimate upward to account for the amplified propagation of a concentrated
sector shock. In practical terms: a 10\% single-day loss in the top-5 AI cluster in
2010 would have cascaded into roughly a 3\% index-level move; the same event in
2026 produces a 7\% index-level impact.

\subsection{Value at Risk and Potential Future Exposure}

Before examining the numerical results, it is worth stating plainly what these
measures mean in practice. Value at Risk (VaR) answers the question: what is the worst
daily loss we should expect to see on 99 out of every 100 trading days? If a 99\% VaR
is 3\%, it means that on a typical bad day, losses should not exceed 3\%. Potential
Future Exposure (PFE) extends this idea across a forward horizon: it traces the
99th-percentile loss trajectory over 252 trading days (one year), accounting for
volatility compounding and scenario-specific drift.

Standard VaR assumes that return distributions are normally shaped. Technology equity
returns are not. They exhibit left skew (large losses are more common than large gains
relative to a symmetric distribution) and excess kurtosis (extremely bad days occur
more often than the Gaussian model predicts). The Cornish-Fisher correction adjusts the
normal quantile $z_\alpha$ for these properties, as derived in Appendix~\ref{app:cf}:

\begin{equation}
  \VaR_{\text{CF}} = -\hat{\mu} + \hat{\sigma} \cdot \zCF
  \label{eq:cf_var_body}
\end{equation}

The Potential Future Exposure at horizon $t$ is the 99th-percentile simulated portfolio
loss across a Monte Carlo ensemble of 2{,}000 GBM paths:

\begin{equation}
  \PFE_t = \text{Quantile}_{99\%}\bigl\{L_t^{(1)}, \ldots, L_t^{(N)}\bigr\}
  \label{eq:pfe}
\end{equation}

where $L_t^{(i)} = 1 - S_t^{(i)} / S_0$ is the fractional loss at horizon $t$ on
simulated path $i$.

\subsection{Model Performance Matrix}

Table~\ref{tab:performance} summarises the IS/OOS backtest results for the
Cornish-Fisher VaR model applied to the Nasdaq-100 and S\&P~500. The IS calibration
window (1995--2021) encompasses the Dotcom crash, the 2008--2009 Financial Crisis, and
the COVID-19 market shock, producing large CF VaR estimates (approximately 6.6\% one-day
at 99\% confidence). The OOS window (2022--2026) experienced the 2022 AI and growth
equity drawdown ($-35.3\%$ NDX peak-to-trough) without producing single-day returns
exceeding the IS-calibrated threshold.

\textbf{Interpretation.} The Kupiec test rejects $H_0$ because the IS-calibrated VaR is
\emph{too conservative} for the 2022--2026 regime. The post-Dotcom and post-GFC era
embedded extreme single-day crashes (e.g., $-12\%$ NDX on 14 March 2000 and $-9.5\%$
on 15 September 2008) into the IS distribution. The AI-era market, by contrast, is
characterised by sustained drawdown velocity rather than single-day spikes. The 2022
decline of $-35\%$ unfolded over 252 trading days, with no individual session exceeding
the IS-calibrated daily threshold.

The Christoffersen independence test does \emph{not} reject $H_0$ for either index.
This confirms that the rare breaches that did occur were serially independent, with no
dangerous clustering. That finding validates the model's regime-discrimination capability
and supports its use as a forward-looking scenario engine.
