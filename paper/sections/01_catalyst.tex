\section{The Catalyst: Capex Overextension and the Compute Cliff}
\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Background: The Infrastructure Spending Cycle}

The current AI infrastructure boom shares structural similarities with the 1990s
telecommunications buildout that precipitated the Dotcom collapse. In both cycles,
a cohort of dominant capital deployers convinced themselves (and their investors) that
the magnitude of long-term network effects justified near-term cash flow destruction.
Between 1997 and 2001, WorldCom, Global Crossing, and Level~3 collectively laid
more than 100{,}000 miles of optical fibre at a capital cost exceeding \$450 billion
\citep{blum2002}. The majority of that capacity remained dark through the end of the decade.

We document three structural parallels between the two cycles:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Exponential capital deployment velocity that decouples from near-term revenue capacity.
  \item Rationalised tolerance for negative free cash flow under a ``land grab'' narrative.
  \item Concentration of deployment among a small cohort of dominant actors whose
        simultaneous stress produces non-linear systemic effects.
\end{enumerate}

What makes the current cycle more dangerous than the fibre-optic era is speed. Cisco,
the canonical poster child of Dotcom overinvestment, scaled its capital expenditure
over roughly six years before the collapse. The four AI hyperscalers analysed here
achieved a comparable absolute spend trajectory in fewer than three years, enabled by
the commoditised availability of GPU hardware and the financing tailwind of near-zero
real interest rates through 2021.

\subsection{The Compute Cliff: Capex-FCF Divergence}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig1_ai_infrastructure_cliff.png}
  \caption{
    \textbf{The AI Infrastructure Cliff (2020--2026).}
    Left panel: annual Capex stacked by hyperscaler (MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, META) sourced
    from SEC 10-K filings \citep{msft_10k_2025,googl_10k_2025,amzn_10k_2025,meta_10k_2025}.
    Right panel: aggregate FCF versus aggregate Capex, showing the crossover
    (the ``jaws'' pattern). The Capex-to-FCF ratio exceeded the structural $1.0\times$
    stress threshold in Q1~2026.
  }
  \label{fig:cliff}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:cliff} illustrates the divergence. Aggregate hyperscaler Capex grew from
\$86 billion in 2020 to \$293 billion in 2025, a trailing three-year compound annual
growth rate of 32.4\%. Free cash flow, constrained by the operating leverage of
GPU-dense data centre construction, grew from \$102 billion to \$127 billion over the
same period (24.5\% cumulative).

Free cash flow is defined as operating cash flow minus capital expenditure:

\begin{equation}
  \text{FCF} = \text{OCF} - \text{Capex}
  \label{eq:fcf}
\end{equation}

The implied \textit{Monetisation Runway} measures how many additional quarters of Capex
growth the current FCF base can sustain before aggregate FCF turns negative. Formally,
given a flat FCF baseline $\overline{F}$ and Capex growing at trailing CAGR $g$:

\begin{equation}
  \text{Runway} = \frac{\ln(\overline{F}\,/\,C_0)}{\ln(1+g)}
  \label{eq:runway}
\end{equation}

where $C_0$ is current annual Capex. With $\overline{F} = \$127$B, $C_0 = \$293$B, and
$g = 32.4\%$, we obtain a runway of approximately 7.3 quarters from Q1~2026. This is a
stress indicator, not a prediction. It signals the point at which, absent a step-change
in AI-driven revenue, hyperscalers begin eroding their cash positions.

\subsection{Historical Parallel: Velocity Comparison with the Dotcom Buildout}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering

  \caption{
    \textbf{Capex Acceleration Velocity: AI Hyperscalers vs.\ Dotcom Telecom (Normalised).}
    Dotcom buildout indexed to Q1~1995~$= 100$ (Cisco CSCO representative). AI buildout
    indexed to Q1~2022~$= 100$ (MSFT, GOOGL, AMZN, META aggregate).
    Source: SEC EDGAR \citep{sec_xbrl_api}, Yahoo Finance \citep{yfinance}.
  }
  \label{fig:dotcom}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:dotcom} normalises capital deployment velocity between the Dotcom
telecom cycle and the current AI buildout. At equivalent time horizons, AI hyperscalers
are deploying capital at approximately $4.3\times$ the normalised velocity of Cisco at
its peak buildout. Two factors drive this acceleration. First, GPU hardware costs have
declined exponentially with each generation, so raw dollar spend translates into
proportionally more physical infrastructure than in the fibre era. Second, the competitive
dynamics of foundation model development create a first-mover pressure that rewards
speed of deployment over capital discipline.

\subsection{The 2026 IPO Liquidity Vacuum}

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fig5_ipo_liquidity_vacuum.png}
  \caption{
    \textbf{2026 Synthetic IPO Supply Timeline.}
    Composite area plot of anticipated equity supply drains from SpaceX, Anthropic,
    and OpenAI public market listings, modelled as a synchronised \$210B capital
    extraction event within the June through December 2026 window
    \citep{bloomberg_spacex_2026,wsj_anthropic_ipo,ft_openai_ipo}.
  }
  \label{fig:ipo_vacuum}
\end{figure}

The \$210 billion aggregate liquidity drain shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ipo_vacuum}
represents approximately 0.8\% of total US public equity market capitalisation. This
figure appears modest in isolation. Its impact is amplified for three compounding reasons.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The shock is concentrated in the AI and technology sector, which constitutes
        approximately 40\% of NDX and 33\% of GSPC index weight.
  \item It arrives simultaneously with the hyperscaler balance sheet stress trigger
        (Capex/FCF exceeding $1.0\times$), providing a fundamental repricing catalyst.
  \item Index concentration ensures that passive vehicle outflows propagate the shock
        to non-AI holdings via forced rebalancing (see Section~\ref{sec:impact}).
\end{enumerate}
