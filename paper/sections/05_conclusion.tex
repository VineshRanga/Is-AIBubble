\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

\subsection{What We Set Out to Prove}

This paper began with a straightforward empirical observation: four companies are
spending more than twice what they earn in free cash flow on data centres, at a pace
that structurally mirrors the fibre-optic overbuild that destroyed trillions of dollars
of market value between 2000 and 2002. We asked three questions. How severe is the
imbalance? What happens when a \$210 billion IPO supply shock collides with that
imbalance at peak market concentration? And do practical tools exist to hedge the
resulting systemic risk before the correction arrives?

\subsection{The Three Empirical Pillars}

The answer to the first question is quantified in Section~\ref{sec:intro}.
The aggregate Capex-to-FCF ratio for the four AI hyperscalers crossed the structural
$1.0\times$ stress threshold in the first quarter of 2026. With FCF flat at \$127
billion and Capex growing at a 32.4\% trailing CAGR, the implied Monetisation Runway
is approximately 7.3 quarters. This is not a prediction of corporate default. It is a
structural indicator that the market is pricing growth expectations that require AI
applications to reach commercial scale before the cash flow math breaks.

The answer to the second question is in Section~\ref{sec:impact}. The simultaneous
public market listing of SpaceX, Anthropic, and OpenAI within a six-month window
constitutes the largest synchronised IPO liquidity extraction in US market history.
At \$210 billion in aggregate float supply, the shock represents a fraction of total
market capitalisation. Its danger is not its absolute size. Its danger is its timing
and its destination. It arrives when hyperscaler balance sheets are at maximum
stress, and it drains liquidity from the precise market segment already under
fundamental pressure.

The amplifier is the HHI concentration factor. The five largest Nasdaq-100
constituents now account for approximately 67.8\% of index weight, compared to
29.2\% in 2010. The HHI-based concentration amplifier of $2.66\times$ means that
a 10\% shock to the AI cluster in 2026 produces a 7\% index-level move that
cascade-forces passive vehicle rebalancing across the entire portfolio. That
cascade is what transforms a sector correction into a systemic event.

\subsection{Why Traditional Diversification Fails Here}

A portfolio manager who understood none of the above but held a standard 60/40
allocation would still be exposed. The mechanism is passive vehicle rebalancing.
When the top-5 NDX constituents command 67.8\% of the index, every broad-market
ETF in the world is, functionally, a concentrated bet on the same five companies.
Holding \textit{VOO} and \textit{QQQ} alongside individual AI positions does not
diversify; it multiplies the concentration. Standard diversification advice fails
when the diversification vehicle itself is the concentrated instrument.

This is the core structural fragility documented in this paper. It is not a risk
that can be analysed away with tighter models. It requires either market structure
reform (concentration guardrails, circuit-breakers) or new instruments that allow
investors to hedge the AI cluster specifically without exiting public equity markets
entirely.

\subsection{Compute Derivatives Are Not a Novelty. They Are a Structural Requirement.}

Section~\ref{sec:mitigation} argues that compute derivatives are the appropriate
corrective instrument. The Ornn Compute Price Index provides the transparent
reference rate that makes derivatives pricing possible. ICE GPU futures allow
hyperscalers to lock in hardware ROI, transforming the binary outcome (monetisation
works or it does not) into a managed variance problem. Prediction markets on IPO
timelines and drawdown thresholds allow institutional investors to hedge specific
event risk within a CFTC-regulated framework.

The quantitative proof of concept in this paper is unambiguous. A 300{,}000-unit
H100 fleet financed with \$9 billion in Capex, hedged via short ICE GPU futures at
the M1 OCPI forward rate, preserves \$528.6 million of cumulative margin over 12
months under a 30\% spot rental rate crash. The hedge effectiveness is 98.4\%.
That is not a rounding error. It represents the difference between a hyperscaler
that manages a compute downturn with discipline and one that faces an unplanned
FCF shortfall mid-cycle.

The broader implication is that compute derivatives markets are not merely useful
risk management tools. They are a structural requirement for the orderly functioning
of public equity markets in an era where AI infrastructure spend has become the
dominant driver of index-level volatility.

\subsection{What This Paper Does Not Claim}

Readers should be clear about the limits of this analysis, which are documented
fully in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}. We do not claim to know when the correction
will occur, or whether it will occur at all. The flat FCF baseline is a stress
scenario, not a central forecast. Revenue upside from AI application adoption
could invalidate the entire runway calculation. Private valuations for the IPO
candidates could compress to 50\% of current estimates, halving the float drain.

The structural argument holds across all plausible parameter combinations. Some
version of a concentrated technology sector experiencing simultaneous IPO supply
pressure is happening in 2026, regardless of the exact dollar figures. The
quantitative framework built here is designed to be re-calibrated as those figures
become observable.

\subsection{A Final Word on Urgency}

The Dotcom collapse was not a surprise to everyone. A meaningful body of academic
and practitioner work identified the structural imbalances in the late 1990s telecom
buildout before the correction arrived. The surprise was not the existence of the
imbalance. The surprise was the speed and severity of the unwind once sentiment
shifted.

The framework in this paper is built to reduce that surprise element for the
current cycle. The Compute Cliff is visible in audited financial statements. The
HHI concentration is computable from published index weights. The IPO supply
pipeline is documented in journalistic and regulatory filings. None of this is
hidden. What has been missing is a unified quantitative framework that connects
these data points to a coherent systemic risk estimate. That is what this paper
provides.

The tools now exist. The decision of whether to use them belongs to market
participants, index providers, and regulators. This paper argues that the cost of
inaction is not theoretical. It is 7.3 quarters away.
